The opening night was a Thursday in late October. Outside, the streets of Bethnal Green were wet with autumn rain, and the orange glow of the streetlamps made everything look slightly suspended, slightly unreal. Inside the gallery — a long converted railway arch with brick walls and high ceilings — fifteen of my paintings hung under warm spotlights, and people were walking among them holding glasses of wine and talking in voices I could not quite hear.

I stood near the back and watched.

---

## How It Happened

The show came about through a connection I had not expected. A curator named Priya Mehta had seen a photograph of one of my paintings — the large *Wheel of Life* I completed in 2023 — in an online group focused on contemporary Buddhist art. She reached out, we exchanged emails over several months, and eventually she proposed a solo exhibition at the Arch Gallery, which she had been running for three years in a space beneath the Bethnal Green railway arches.

I had shown work before, in Dharamsala and once in Delhi, but London was different. London was my first time showing outside India, my first encounter with an audience that had no prior context for the imagery — no childhood exposure to monasteries, no grandmother who turned a prayer wheel, no sense of what it means when a deity has many arms.

This both frightened me and made me curious.

---

## The Fifteen Paintings

I showed three categories of work:

**Traditional thangkas** — a *Green Tara*, a *Thousand-Armed Chenrezig*, and a *Medicine Buddha* (Sangye Menla). These were the most technically demanding pieces, completed over many months using traditional mineral pigments and gold. I included them because I wanted visitors to understand the tradition I come from — what the grammar is, before I show them where I have taken it.

**Contemporary work in the thangka vocabulary** — six paintings that use the geometric grids, the mineral pigments, the precise line work of traditional painting, but apply them to images that do not appear in any iconographic manual: the Barkhor market at dawn, the windows of apartment blocks in Dharamsala, a map of the route my family took out of Tibet. These felt like the most personal things I have ever made.

**Works on paper** — six small works, each about the size of a notebook, made during a residency I did in the Kinnaur valley in Himachal Pradesh in the summer of 2024. Sketches, in a sense, but finished ones — studies of light on high-altitude rock, of cloud formations, of the specific quality of green that appears in a valley at 3,000 metres.

---

## What People Asked

The questions on the opening night and through the two-week run of the show surprised me.

Many visitors asked whether a non-Buddhist could commission a thangka, or whether owning one required certain religious obligations. The answer is no — thangkas have been made as devotional objects, as teaching aids, and as works of art throughout their history. You do not need to be Buddhist to appreciate one, or to own one.

Several people asked whether I felt any conflict between making traditional religious art and making contemporary work. I found this the most interesting question of the show, and I gave different answers on different days. The honest answer, I think, is that the conflict I feel is not between tradition and modernity but between patience and urgency — the thangka demands absolute slowness, while everything I feel about my subject matter feels pressing, immediate.

One older man stood in front of my painting of the Barkhor for a very long time. When I introduced myself as the artist, he told me he had been in Lhasa in 1983, on a tour organised by the Chinese government, and that the market looked exactly as I had painted it. We talked for twenty minutes. He cried a little. I did too.

---

## What I Brought Home

I came back to Dharamsala with four things: a small profit (enough to buy new lapis and a better grinding stone), a residency invitation for 2026 from a gallery in Frankfurt, a folder full of notes for new paintings, and a feeling I am still working out how to name.

It is something like this: I had thought that showing my work in London would be a kind of translation exercise — finding a way to explain Tibetan art to people who had no context for it. What I discovered instead is that the work mostly explains itself. The Wheel of Life needed no caption. The map of my family's route across the Himalayas needed no explanation. People stood in front of these images and felt something, and what they felt was not so different from what I feel when I paint.

I do not know what to call that — the place where one person's private language becomes, somehow, legible to strangers. But I think it might be what art is.
