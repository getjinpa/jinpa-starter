The alarm does not go off. I wake at five-thirty or six most mornings without one, which I attribute less to discipline than to the fact that in Dharamsala the crows begin before dawn, followed almost immediately by the first round of loudspeaker prayers from the monastery three streets over. You learn to incorporate these sounds rather than resist them.

---

## The First Hour

I make tea in the kitchen — Tibetan butter tea when I have the ingredients, otherwise strong black Assam — and carry it to the table by the east-facing window. In winter, this is still dark. In spring and summer, I can see the Dhauladhar range from here, the ridge of the Himalayas that backs Dharamsala like a theatre flat, painted in whatever colour the morning has decided on.

I spend the first hour or so not painting. I sit, I drink tea, I look at the painting in progress on the easel. This is not procrastination — it is the most important part of the day. I am reacquainting myself with the painting, the way you reacquaint yourself with a long book after setting it aside for a night. I am finding the thread.

The painting currently on my easel is a large Thousand-Armed Chenrezig — about 80 by 60 centimetres, on cotton canvas stretched over a frame. I began it in late November and I estimate it will be finished in April. There are eleven heads, the forty main arms in their correct positions with their specific attributes, and the remaining nine hundred and sixty arms rendered as a kind of halo. Each arm holds something: a lotus, a water vessel, a rope, a bow, a vase. Each gesture has a meaning. The work of tracking these, of consulting the texts, of measuring the grid lines with a ruler and compass, is meticulous in a way that I find, paradoxically, very calming.

---

## The Working Hours

I start painting at around seven-thirty. There is a window I need to catch — the light from the east-facing window is best from about eight to eleven, before it shifts and the quality of illumination changes. In the afternoon I work in lamp light, which has a warmer tone and suits different kinds of work.

I begin with the parts of the painting that need the most precision: the line work, the geometric measurements, the basic lay-in of the major pigment areas. By this stage of the Chenrezig, I am working on the intermediate arms and their attributes, which requires me to hold a certain kind of attention — focused but not tense, present but patient.

My brushes are made of varying materials. The finest detail work — the lines of a face, the patterns on a robe, the outlines of the smallest attributes — is done with a brush made from a single hair of a cat's ear, set into a wooden handle I have used for twelve years. It is, I am aware, an unusual implement. My teacher Kelsang Wangdu gave it to me at the end of my training. He told me it was made for him by his teacher, and that his teacher had said: *"Any tool that helps you pay attention is a good tool."*

---

## Midday

I stop at noon or one o'clock, eat lunch (usually dal and rice, often carried up from the small restaurant at the end of my street, whose owner knows I am working and leaves a portion for me without being asked), and rest for forty-five minutes. This break is essential. The eye becomes unreliable after several hours of fine work. You stop seeing what is actually there and start seeing what you think should be there, which is a different thing entirely.

In the afternoon I often do preparatory work for future pieces: grinding pigments, sizing new canvases, sketching compositions, reading. I keep a library of iconographic texts — some in Tibetan, some in English translations — and spend time most days with them. Understanding what you are painting, and why, seems to me inseparable from painting it well.

---

## The Evening

By four or five in the afternoon the light has changed enough that I stop. I clean my brushes — a meditative ritual in itself, requiring patience and the right sequence of cleaning agents — and prepare the palette for the next morning.

In the evenings I read, write (including, occasionally, for this blog), and walk. Dharamsala is a walking city — the hills demand it, and the views reward it. I often walk up toward the Bhagsu waterfall or along the ridge path toward Triund, depending on the season. The mountains are there, as they always are. They have been here longer than any painting.

I find that the best ideas for new work come not when I am at the easel but when I am walking, or in the early morning before I begin. The actual painting is the execution of something that has already happened somewhere else, in some other register of attention.

I do not know where that register is. I find it every morning, and I am grateful for it every time.
