My earliest memory of paint is not of paint at all — it is of dust.

I am seven years old, sitting cross-legged on the floor of my uncle Lobsang's studio in the Barkhor neighbourhood of Lhasa. The room smells of juniper smoke and something mineral, something ancient. He is grinding a small blue stone in circles against a flat piece of slate, his hands steady, patient, as if he has all the time in the world. And perhaps he does. The stone is lapis lazuli, brought from Badakhshan in Afghanistan along routes that have not changed in a thousand years. What he is making, slowly, grain by grain, is the colour of the sky in a thangka painting.

I did not know then that I would one day do the same thing. I did not know many things.

---

## Leaving and Arriving

When my family crossed into Nepal in 1994, I was eleven. I carried a small cloth bag my grandmother had pressed into my hands the night before. Inside were two things: a photograph of the Potala Palace, and a drawing I had made of the Jokhang Temple — the first drawing I had ever been proud of.

We eventually settled in Dharamsala, in the hill station in the Indian state of Himachal Pradesh that has become the seat of the Tibetan government-in-exile. I grew up watching mountains disappear into clouds and listening to monks debate in courtyards. Art felt both necessary and impossible — necessary because it connected me to everything I had left behind, impossible because who was I to make it?

The question answered itself slowly, the way most important questions do.

## The Norbulingka Institute

At twenty-three, I enrolled in the thangka painting programme at the Norbulingka Institute, a centre for Tibetan arts and crafts just outside Dharamsala. My teacher was Kelsang Wangdu, then seventy years old, who had trained in the Menri tradition — one of the oldest schools of Tibetan painting — and who had a way of watching your hand that made you feel he could see everything you had ever been afraid of.

Thangka painting is not taught the way Western art is taught. There is no encouragement to find your own style, no celebration of the spontaneous gesture. You begin by learning to draw geometric grids. Weeks of nothing but grids, perfectly spaced, perfectly measured. When I asked Kelsang why, he smiled and said: *"You must learn the structure before you can forget it."*

The structure of a thangka — the precise placement of a deity's eyes, the angle of a hand, the proportions of a mandala — is not arbitrary. Every measurement has been refined over centuries, transmitted from master to student, encoded in texts like the *Menri Ratna Mandala*. When you paint a figure correctly, you are not expressing yourself. You are participating in something much larger.

It took me years to understand that this was a form of freedom, not a constraint.

## The Materials

The pigments we use are mostly minerals: lapis lazuli, malachite, cinnabar, orpiment, white lead, carbon black. Some are crushed stones, others are prepared through elaborate processes involving heat, acids, and time. They are mixed with a sizing solution made from yak hide glue and water. Applied to cotton canvas that has been stretched, sized, and burnished smooth with a stone.

Gold is used for outlines and highlights. Not gold paint — actual gold, ground to a fine powder and mixed with the sizing, then applied with a brush and burnished bright with a hard stone until it catches the light like the sun on a monastery roof.

The process is slow. A complex thangka can take six months to a year. This is not considered a problem.

---

## What I Paint Now

I still paint traditional thangkas — Chenrezig, Tara, the Wheel of Life — and I will always paint them. But over the past decade I have also been making a separate body of work that takes the vocabulary of thangka and uses it to paint things my teachers never painted: the city of Lhasa as I remember it, the refugee camp where my cousin was born, the face of my grandmother the way I see it in dreams.

This is not iconoclasm. I am not trying to break anything. I am trying to find out whether the structure can hold new things — new sorrows, new landscapes, new forms of longing.

So far, I believe it can.
