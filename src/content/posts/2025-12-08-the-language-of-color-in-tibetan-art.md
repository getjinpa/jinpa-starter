Someone once asked me, after looking at one of my paintings for a long time, why Tara is green.

It is a good question. She could have been any colour. In some traditions she appears in white, in red, in blue. But the most widely painted form of Tara — Green Tara, *Sgrol-ljang* in Tibetan — is almost always depicted in a vivid, slightly warm green. The question of why opens a door into something I find endlessly fascinating: the grammar of colour in Tibetan Buddhist art.

In this tradition, colour is not decoration. It is language.

---

## The Five Buddha Colors

At the heart of Tibetan Buddhist iconography is a system of five colours, each corresponding to one of the five Dhyani Buddhas — the five aspects of enlightened mind. Understanding these is the foundation of reading a thangka.

**White** is the colour of Vairochana, the Buddha of the centre. White corresponds to the transformation of ignorance into the wisdom of the Dharmadhatu — the all-encompassing space of reality. In paintings, deities depicted in white are associated with purity, healing, pacification. White Tara embodies long life and compassion; Avalokiteshvara (Chenrezig) often appears in white.

**Yellow/Gold** is the colour of Ratnasambhava, the jewel-born Buddha of the south. It represents the transformation of pride into the wisdom of equanimity. Gold, in particular, carries the warmth of abundance and the richness of earth. When you see gold flames around a deity, or a golden throne, you are seeing a visual statement about the inexhaustible nature of enlightenment.

**Red** corresponds to Amitabha, the Buddha of the west, and the transformation of attachment into discriminating wisdom. Red is power, magnetism, compassionate action. Wrathful deities who appear in red are often performing acts of fierce compassion — removing obstacles, purifying negativity. Hayagriva, the horse-headed deity, blazes in red.

**Green** is the colour of Amoghasiddhi, the Buddha of the north, and the transformation of jealousy into all-accomplishing wisdom. Green is activity, energy, the swift movement of enlightened action. This is why Green Tara is green — she is the embodiment of swift, active compassion. She does not wait. She moves toward suffering.

**Blue/Black** is the colour of Akshobhya, the imperturbable Buddha of the east, and the transformation of anger into mirror-like wisdom. The darkest blues — almost black — are used for the most powerful wrathful deities. Mahakala, the great black protector, wears this colour. Blue denotes stability, indestructibility, the deep quiet of a sky after a storm.

## Colours of the Mandala

When a painter creates a mandala — the sacred geometric diagram that represents the palace of a deity — colour serves a structural purpose as well as a symbolic one. The four cardinal directions of a mandala are conventionally coloured:

- **East** (usually at the bottom): White
- **South** (right): Yellow
- **West** (top): Red
- **North** (left): Green
- **Centre**: Blue or Gold

To paint a mandala correctly, you must know not only which colour goes where but why — the cosmology that organises the universe into a pattern of transformations.

## Pigments and Spirit

One of the things that struck me most when I began studying thangka painting was that the pigments are not merely functional — they carry meaning in their origins.

Lapis lazuli comes from high-altitude mines in Afghanistan. For centuries it was one of the most expensive substances in the world; in Renaissance Europe it was reserved for the robes of the Virgin Mary. In Tibetan art it paints the sky of enlightenment, the robes of Shakyamuni Buddha, the faces of certain wrathful deities.

Malachite, the green mineral copper carbonate, was ground to create the warm green of Tara's skin. Cinnabar — mercury sulphide — gave the blazing red of Hayagriva. White lead was used for highlights, for the crests of waves in an ocean of milk, for the whites of eyes.

These materials come from the earth, transformed by time and pressure into specific colours. There is something right about using them to describe states of mind that are also transformed by time and practice.

---

## A Living Language

Colour conventions in Tibetan art are not arbitrary, and they are not decorative. But they are also not static. Different lineages and regional traditions have their own variations. Some schools paint Mahakala dark blue rather than black. Some depictions of Chenrezig show him in red. Context, lineage, and the specific practice for which a thangka is commissioned all affect the palette.

What remains constant is the principle: every colour is chosen, and every choice means something. When you look at a thangka, you are not only looking at a painting. You are reading a text in a language of colour that has been refined over a thousand years.

I have been studying this language for most of my adult life, and I am still, in many ways, only beginning.
