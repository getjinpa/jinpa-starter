The Tibetan name is *Chenrezig* (སྤྱན་རས་གཟིགས). The Sanskrit is *Avalokiteshvara*. The translation most commonly offered in English is *the one who looks down with compassion*, though I was once told by a scholar that a more precise rendering would be *the lord who sees with his eyes in every direction*. This is the bodhisattva — a being who has deferred their own enlightenment in order to remain in the world and reduce the suffering of all sentient beings.

In the Thousand-Armed form, *Sahasrabhuja Avalokiteshvara*, this figure is depicted with eleven heads (representing the ability to see suffering in eleven directions simultaneously, or, in some interpretations, the eleven stages of the bodhisattva path) and a thousand arms. The hands of the forty main arms each hold a specific attribute: a lotus, a wish-fulfilling jewel, a rope, a vase, a bow and arrow, a willow branch. In each of the remaining palms is an eye.

I have been painting this figure since late November.

---

## Why This Form

The Thousand-Armed Chenrezig is considered one of the most complex figures in the Tibetan iconographic tradition. It took me several years before I felt prepared to attempt it.

The decision came from two directions. One was a commission: a family in Dharamsala, whose father had practised this particular form of Chenrezig for forty years, asked me to paint it as a memorial for him after his death in the spring of 2025. The other was personal. I had reached a point in my practice where I needed to challenge myself at a level I had not yet tried — not technically only, but in terms of concentration and stamina. A painting that requires four to six months of steady daily work, with very little room for error, is a test of something deeper than skill.

---

## The Grid

Every thangka begins with a geometric grid drawn on the sized canvas. This grid — based on proportional systems developed in Tibetan iconometric texts, particularly the *Bris ston nor bu'i me long* — dictates the placement of every major element: the position of the deity's navel, the width of the face, the length of the upper arms, the angle of the knee.

For the Thousand-Armed form, the grid is considerably more complex than for a single-figured composition. The forty main arms radiate outward from the body in precise arcs; their positions are determined by a series of overlapping circles drawn from the central axis. The remaining arms form a fan shape that fills the entire upper portion of the composition, each one slightly different in angle, each one carrying its eye.

I spent two weeks on the grid alone.

## Gold

I am using three types of gold in this painting. Shell gold — fine gold powder mixed with the sizing solution — for the areas of flat gold, the ornaments, the throne, the halo. Line gold — the same preparation, applied with the finest brush — for outlines and the delicate patterns on the robes. And burnished gold for the highlights, applied thickly and then rubbed smooth and bright with a hard agate stone until it catches the light at certain angles with a sharpness that no paint can achieve.

Working with gold requires a different quality of attention. The sizing solution has an optimal window — too wet and the gold lifts; too dry and it will not adhere. You have to time your work to the drying cycle of the medium, which changes with temperature and humidity. In summer, in Dharamsala, this is a particular challenge. In winter it is better.

The gold for this painting came from a supplier in Kathmandu who sources it from traditional producers in Nepal. It arrives as a very fine powder in small glass vials. I have seven vials. I expect to use five.

## What I Am Learning

Four months in, I can report that the painting is teaching me things I did not expect.

The experience of painting a thousand arms is, in part, an experience of repetition. Each arm is different — different angle, different attribute, different length — but the action of painting them is fundamentally the same action, repeated again and again. You might think this would become mechanical. Instead I find that it becomes contemplative. The repetition creates a kind of open space in the mind, similar to what I understand to be the intention behind certain forms of meditation practice.

The eyes in the palms, which I have begun painting now, are the strangest part. Each palm has an eye at its centre — small, precise, looking outward in its particular direction. Painting the first twenty I found unsettling, in a way I cannot fully explain. By the fiftieth I had stopped feeling unsettled and started feeling watched, which is not the same thing.

The figure of Chenrezig sees in every direction simultaneously. That is the point of the form. That is what compassion is, in this conception: not directed attention, not charitable impulse, but an omnidirectional seeing — a regard that excludes nothing and no one.

---

## Completion

I expect to finish this painting in late April 2026. I will post progress images here as I go, and a full documentation of the finished work.

The family who commissioned it will receive it on the first anniversary of the father's passing, in May. I have met them twice. They are quiet, careful people who take the commission with great seriousness. The father, they told me, used to sit in front of his old, damaged Chenrezig — a small printed thangka from which the gold had long since faded — for an hour each morning, reciting the six-syllable mantra: *Om mani padme hum.*

I think about him when I am working. The mantra runs through my head while I paint. This is, I suppose, what it means for a painting to be devotional — not that it depicts a deity, but that something passes through the making of it that is difficult to name.
